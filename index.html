E-mergency of Memory

Archiving Grief in the Age of Platform Precarity

A mixed-methods study of memorialized Facebook and Instagram accounts as emergent sites of collective grief, cultural heritage, and community-built preservation.

1 | The Emergency

A cemetery governed by Terms of Service.

The project begins from a stark cultural shift: social profiles often outlive their owners. Condolence threads become rolling memorial books, birthdays become recurring rituals, and final profile images begin to function as digital relics.

Yet these memorial spaces are held inside commercial platforms whose policies, algorithms, privacy settings, and business futures can change without an archival duty to the bereaved. The study names this condition as an emergency of memory.

3 | Methodology

A corpus built for both computation and close reading.

The research combines computational text analysis with qualitative interpretation, treating grief online as both data and lived cultural practice.

27 Facebook accounts
23 Instagram accounts
2020-2024 collection period
180 posts close-read

Sentiment Pipeline

VADER scores were paired with a fine-tuned RoBERTa model for grief-specific registers. Cohen's kappa reached 0.74.

Topic Modelling

Gensim LDA identified five coherent discourse themes, optimized at k = 5 with Cv = 0.58.

Grounded Reading

Close reading of 180 posts developed categories for sacralization, platform anxiety, and digital solidarity.

Research Ethics

Accounts were anonymized and handled under AoIR 2020 guidance as public data with contextual integrity.

Key Finding

Memorial precarity anxiety names grief under platform distrust.

The research identifies a grief-specific emotional register: posts that combine ordinary remembrance with urgent fear that a platform may delete, hide, privatize, or otherwise destabilize the memorial.

"Archive everything. Share this so people know."

These posts often carry elevated anxiety despite otherwise restorative memorial language, revealing how grief is intensified by infrastructural uncertainty.

5 | Discussion

Critical Care turns grassroots preservation into a governance model.

The model moves from what communities already do toward the institutional and policy support needed for digital memory to survive beyond platform goodwill.

Grassroots | Fluid | Temporal

Emergent Practice

Validate the already-existing labour of saving screenshots, downloading posts, copying captions, and sharing fragile traces before a platform can erase them.

  • Recognize community archivists as cultural memory workers.
  • Stop penalizing preservation practices as suspicious platform behaviour.
  • Preserve context, not only isolated images or files.

6 | Implications

The study speaks to three audiences.

Digital Humanists

Memorialized accounts should be treated as legitimate cultural heritage objects requiring archival care.

Platform Designers

Designing for grief requires a different logic than designing for ranking, traffic, and engagement.

Policy Makers

Digital remains need regulatory attention because commercial goodwill is not a preservation framework.

"The emergency of memory is already here. The response must be equally emergent."
